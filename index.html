A Study on Privacy

How does the day-to-day use of technology affect our privacy?


Prologue

Society has come to largely rely on the use of modern devices to the point of them being near essential for daily living, getting by without using a modern smart device has become near impossible. This has given the power to information greater than any other time in history, large companies now have easier and more access to our data and also can profit off of it easier and more than they ever have before. We believe that this made calls for a look into an understanding of privacy and information, for we feel that it’s not okay for large corporations to entirely abuse their privileges and entrench our information in the way that they do. For this project, we investigated how these companies gather our data, what data they’re gathering, and the outcomes and implications that may arise from their data-gathering methods. We found this all to be important, as having this information allowed us to come to a sound, educated, and relatively objective conclusion on our moral and ethical standpoints around the subject.

Opportunities and Society

Privacy has been a concern of large contention throughout the last decade, with data leaks and information invasiveness scandals constantly on the headlines. It’s well known that even the United States government in particular amasses a vast amount of data that should otherwise be personal and private[1]. The argument is made that a person who has nothing to hide shouldn’t care about the invasiveness of these companies, but that’s a very surface-level notion that I believe doesn’t take a proper, critical look at the larger picture. I’m sure we all enjoy our shower curtains, and I’m sure we’d all feel uncomfortable with a camera and microphone shoved in our face while we eat, sleep and breathe all day. And I’m sure we’d appreciate it if companies simply let us know what and when they’re gathering our information, and what it’s used for. Unfortunately, a majority of people who deal with data, aren’t very interested in the moral and ethical implications that this data-gathering concerns.

Privacy has turned into such a great concern that many countries have had to enact laws and regulations in order to protect the population’s data privacy. A famous example is Europe’s GDPR (General Data Protection Regulations), which has aided in regulating the use of data tracking and gathering of personal information. This regulation made it so that companies operating within the EU are obligated to take measures in letting users know when their information is being tracked and for their permission before using these websites, they’re also obligated to let you know if a large data breach has occurred, among many other regulations.

In New Zealand, large companies that regularly operate in Europe have to follow these guidelines, however, companies that operate within New Zealand follow a different set of rules[2] under the Privacy Act[3]. This act is similar to Europe’s GDPR in that you have to state when and what information you’re gathering, and that you only gather what’s needed. This privacy act applies to the Government, Companies, Small businesses, Social clubs and any other type of organisation. On top of this, the act applies different rules and regulations for certain information. Organisations which hold information on credit, health, civil defence, telecommunications, superannuation, or the justice sector all fall under different regulations to uphold as opposed to organisations which hold more typical information like interests and browsing history.
